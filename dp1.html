PINKY BABE

An interactive robotic creature inspired by traffic cones and transformed into a friendly bird-like companion.

Overview

Inspired by the geometric shape of an urban traffic cone, Pinky Babe reinterprets a cold, warning symbol into a soft, adorable bird-like robot. Rather than focusing on high-tech mechanical visual language, the project prioritizes human-robot interaction through emotional companion design, tactile materials, and expressive behaviors.

Background
Developed as a study in Human-Robot Interaction (HRI) to explore how physical textures and zoomorphic behaviors mitigate mechanical anxiety.
Concept

A Friendly Bird-Like Companion

The pyramidal structure originates from the geometry of a traffic cone. Through visual abstraction and soft materialization, the cone becomes a clumsy, adorable bird-like creature.

By shifting proportions to mimic baby-like traits (a large head, wide eyes, and a soft body envelope), the design reduces the user's perception of machinery and builds a warm, approachable personality.

Details

Component Design Details

Surface Treatment

The robot is wrapped in colorful yarn, creating a soft, tactile texture. This surface allows burr seeds to naturally attach during movement, symbolizing outdoor interaction.

Crest Design

A red flower sits on the head. A constantly glowing light bulb at its center acts as an emotional focal point and increases character recognition.

Wing Design

Both wings house internal light bulbs pulsing to musical beats. Exposed yarn tails highlight the warm, hand-crafted quality of the companion.

Face & Structure

Adopts baby-like facial proportions. Transparent acrylic frames balance the vivid color palette, making the structure look lightweight and gentle.

Choreography

Motion & Performance Design

To establish a convincing Human-Robot Interaction, we developed a synchronized motion sequence mapping light intensity, wing flaps, and wheel movement to coordinate with the background music. We addressed challenges related to sound pacing and physical stage conditions through extensive testing.

Dynamic Light & Gesture Sync

Our assigned soundtrack was relatively flat with no dramatic rhythmic changes. To make the interaction feel organic, we programmed subtle breathing pulses in the head crest light bulb and mapped wing flap frequencies to gentle tempo indicators.

Locomotion & Venue Calibration

The coupling between the wheeled base and the performance stage surface was highly unpredictable, introducing wobbles and slippage. We designed adaptive speed calibration patterns and performed iterative motion tests on matching terrains to ensure path accuracy.

Challenges

Encountered Problems & Solutions

Problem 01

Weight Distribution & Locomotion Stability

The high center of gravity caused by the thick knitted yarn wrap and top-heavy companion structure led to severe wobbling during movement. By replacing inner plates with lightweight laser-cut transparent acrylic frames and stabilizing the base chassis, we lowered the center of gravity and stabilized its locomotion.

Problem 02

Knitted Material Friction & Servo Stalling

The organic yarn-knitted envelope added unexpected weight and motion resistance to the moving wing joints, causing the micro servos to stall or jitter. We resolved this by optimizing the joint spacing, reducing the yarn thickness around the wings' pivot points, and adjusting the servo torque parameters in the code.

Problem 03

Inner Space Constraints & Cable Routing

Housing microcontrollers, battery packs, motors, and glowing LED modules inside the compact bird-like frame created wire tension and risk of short circuits. We designed a modular vertical chassis layout and mapped clean routing paths within the acrylic frames to organize the internal architecture.

Conclusion

Key Takeaways

Building a physical HRI project brought key insights into how audiences project empathy onto inanimate objects. When movement patterns match emotional cues, the robot's physical form transitions from a simple machine to a character with agency.

Design Insights

Learned how physical behaviors and material textures directly influence audience trust and their perception of a robot's personality. Handmade flaws (like exposed yarn) actually make robots feel more accessible than standard polished plastic enclosures.
